About

I just finished my contract at Capital One as a UX/UI Developer. At Capital One, I worked with designers and brought their designs to life by coding them in HTML, CSS (Sass) and Angular. My average day was spent coding, coming up with solutions to design problems and communicating with designers.



Previously I worked at Caterpillar inc, where I worked on an international team of designers and developers. They brought us all together to build the fourth-generation operating system's user interface. We designed all of our work in Illustrator and coded them as Web Components.



Before working at these two companies, I started my career a decade ago as a UX/UI designer and worked my way into being a UX/UI developer.



Now Reading

Title
SVG Animations
Author
Sarah Drasner

This is an excellent book. It starts off going through understanding the viewBox and preserveAspectRatio. It then goes into teaching you how to animate SVG's with CSS.



One of the things I enjoyed about this book is that it covers animations without libraries. This is a really cool thing to do because we know that libraries come and go. Plus that they add on extra code when being placed into your application.



With that being said, the book does go into animating SVG's with GreenSock, React, and Mo.js also. So overall, if you at all like you and me are dealing with SVG animations. Then a book like this could be instrumental in understanding the mechanics underneath how SVG's animate.

  1. Hear the proposition or business idea - convert it to one sentence
  2. User Research: Competitors and spreadsheet analysis
  3. User Research: Find the gaps and discover a competitive advantage
  4. User Research: Make user personas and mood boards
  5. User Research: User Interviews
  6. Move forward or stop: evaluate whether this is a good idea or not
  7. Create the business / marketing strategy
  8. Website or Web Application architecture - sitemaps to Web application components
  9. Wireframe the interface design
  10. Follow or create a design system
  11. Design the interface in Sketch
  12. Code (HTML 5, CSS 3, JavaScript, SVG) or create (InVision Studio) an interactive prototype
  13. Share the prototype with shareholders for final feedback before handing off to the engineers
  14. Once given the approval, collaborate with the engineering team towards project completion
Your a UX Designer, what made you want to learn how to code?
Close
As a UX Designer, I would make these perfect designs that the share holders would love. I would then hand them over to the developers, who would then tell me that they could not code them the way I designed them. At the time, I realized I was working with a lot of Java developers who hated CSS. So I took it upon myself to complete the designs instead of just handing over the designs.

What a difference it has made in my career. I am no longer just a UX Designer, I am also a UX Developer.
What is the best thing that has happened in your career?
Close
FAILING! Failing in my career is the best thing that ever happened to me. For so long I was winning at everything. I designed (and coded) mobile apps that made a lot of money, designed a children's hospital, designed a trade-show and even a large scale commercial.

I could do no wrong and then it came crashing down. Failure grounds us, humbles us and if you have any sense of dignity after your failure... you learn from it and become a better version of yourself.
